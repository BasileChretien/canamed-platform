Two medical schools. The same patient. A conversation worth comparing.

CANAMED brings medical students from partner universities into one shared online room for problem-based learning — a clinical case, a breaking-bad-news roleplay, and the team decisions they have to make together. The platform structures the session, rewards the reasoning, and turns each difference between teaching cultures into a teaching moment.

  • Two traditions, one room

    Rooms are balanced across partner universities, so different teaching cultures meet face to face on the same case.

  • Reasoning, not speed

    Points reward the order of decisions, the depth of the answers and the team's vote on the big calls. Wrong choices are explained.

  • Research-grade by design

    Structured contributions, shared answers and clean exports — every session feeds the underlying education-research project.

A research collaboration between partner universities. Open source · no account required.

      Country compare — opioids & chronic pain

      Per-capita opioid consumption differs sharply between countries:

      • Japan: among the lowest opioid consumption of high-income countries.
      • France: moderate — well below the USA, above Japan.
      • USA: historically very high, with a well-known opioid crisis.

      Guidelines for chronic non-specific low-back pain are broadly similar across countries (stay active, exercise, limit imaging, opioids not first-line) — but prescribing culture, regulation and patient expectations differ sharply. The guidelines barely differ — so any difference you find between France and Japan is cultural and regulatory, not scientific. That is exactly what this module is about. Discuss: is lower opioid use protective, or does it risk under-treating pain?

                  📚 Background & guidelines (open when you need them)

                  📜 Historical context — how we got here

                  Opium is one of medicine's oldest drugs — used in ancient Sumer, praised by Galen, bottled as laudanum from the 17th century. The 19th century industrialised it: morphine was isolated around 1804, the hypodermic syringe arrived in the 1850s, and Bayer marketed heroin in 1898. Repeated waves of addiction made 20th-century Western physicians cautious — opioids were for cancer and acute pain only.

                  That caution collapsed in the United States. In 1995–96 pain was promoted as "the fifth vital sign" and OxyContin was launched with aggressive, misleading marketing; prescriptions tripled and hundreds of thousands of overdose deaths followed. Japan took a strikingly different path — tight narcotics regulation, restrictive insurance rules, conservative training and a deep cultural wariness of mayaku (narcotics) — and today prescribes opioids many times less than the US, with France and the UK in between. Low-back-pain care had its own reversal: until the 1990s bed rest and routine imaging were standard, until trials showed bed rest delays recovery and imaging rarely changes outcomes.

                  💡 Did you know? Heroin was sold over the counter as a "non-addictive" cough remedy from 1898 — a marketing claim eerily echoed a century later for OxyContin.

                  💡 Did you know? Japan prescribes opioids more than 20× less than the USA — not because Japanese patients feel less pain, but because of regulation, insurance and culture.

                  • UK — NICE, Low back pain and sciatica in over 16s: assessment and management, NG59 (2016, updated 2020). nice.org.uk/guidance/ng59

                  All four agree on the science: stay active, exercise-based first-line care, image only for red flags, opioids are not first-line for chronic non-specific low-back pain.

                  First-line management of chronic non-specific low-back pain

                  • Education & reassurance — not dangerous, not "damage"; hurt ≠ harm; good prognosis.
                  • Stay active — avoid bed rest; resume work, walking, gym.
                  • Structured exercise / physiotherapy — the best-evidenced treatment; adherence matters more than the type.
                  • Address yellow flags — fear of movement, catastrophising, low mood, poor sleep — treat these directly.
                  • Non-opioid analgesia — NSAIDs at the lowest effective dose, shortest duration. Paracetamol alone is ineffective.
                  • Do NOT — routine imaging, opioids, gabapentinoids / benzodiazepines, prolonged bed rest.

                  Opioids for chronic non-cancer pain

                  Small short-term pain benefit at best, no proven benefit on function, no good evidence beyond ~12 weeks; real harms (tolerance, dependence, hyperalgesia, falls, overdose). All four countries: not first-line.

                  Module B — Breaking Bad News: A Cross-Cultural Roleplay

                  The situation (read once, together). Mr / Mrs Tanaka-Martin, 60 years old, came in for tiredness and weight loss. The tests are back: the diagnosis is a serious, chronic, life-changing illness — your group chooses one (for example: a newly diagnosed cancer, advanced heart failure, early Parkinson's disease, or kidney failure needing dialysis). It is treatable but not curable, and it will change how the patient lives. The patient does not yet know. Their adult child has come with them and is in the room — and at some point will quietly ask you something.

                  Before you start — two things.

                  This is a simulation. The feelings can still feel real. If anything hits too close to home, you can move into the observer role at any time, no explanation needed. When you debrief, talk about the physician's choices — never criticise the person.

                  English is everyone's second or third language here — that is part of the point. If you cannot find a word, slow down, use a simpler one, or pause. Silence is allowed in this conversation and often helps. Empathy is in your tone, your pace and your face, not in advanced vocabulary.

                  Pick your role for this roleplay. Everyone not in a named role is an observer (timekeeper + Phase 3 facilitator).

                  Two of you picked the same role — decide together so everyone has a part.

                  Round 1 — first run

                  SPIKES — Setting · Perception (what do they already understand?) · Invitation (how much do they want to know?) · Knowledge (news in small, plain pieces) · Emotions (name and acknowledge the feeling) · Strategy (agree the next step; you won't abandon them)
                  Useful sentences (English is everyone's second language): "I'm afraid I have some serious news." · "Would you like me to explain everything, or focus on what we do next?" · "I can see this is hard to hear." · "Take your time." · "What questions do you have for me?"
                  📝 Observer's SPIKES checklist — tick what you see, note one win + one hard moment

                  Just for you — tick a step when the physician does it, and jot the moments you'll raise in the debrief. Saved on this device only.

                  When the family asks you to withhold information — "Pause · Explore · Explain · Realign"
                  1. Pause — don't refuse or agree on the spot. Thank them; their worry is real and usually loving.
                  2. Explore the why — "Help me understand what worries you most about your parent knowing."
                  3. Explain your position — you cannot deceive a patient who wants to know, but you do not have to force information on someone who does not.
                  4. Realign — ask the patient how much they want to know and who they want involved (this is SPIKES' "Invitation"). The family's worry becomes part of the conversation, not a secret kept from the patient.

                  Ground rule (an observer reads this aloud): we are comparing practices and how they are changing, not ranking countries. Neither model is "the advanced one". Speak about what you have actually seen or been taught — and it is completely fine to say "I'm not sure, this is changing."

                  An observer reads these out one at a time — for each, make sure both a Caen and a Nagoya voice answers:

                  1. Who is the information for? When there is serious news, who is told first — the patient or the family? Is that what the law says, the textbook says, or what actually happens on the ward? Name one way France and Japan are the same and one way they differ.
                  2. Has it changed? Was disclosure done differently 20–30 years ago? (Japan: cancer-diagnosis disclosure rose from under ~30% in the early 1990s to ~94% by 2016. France: the 2002 loi Kouchner changed patients' information rights.) Why do norms change — law, generations, expectations?
                  3. The family's quiet request — what did you actually do, and why? Would a typical doctor in France respond the same way as one in Japan? What is each most worried about — lying to the patient, or harming the patient? Is "protecting someone from bad news" love, or taking away their choice — or both?
                  4. Autonomy vs. family — is it really either/or? France is described as "autonomy first", Japan as "family-centred" — but French law also protects the right not to know, and many Japanese patients today want full information. Where does your country actually sit? What would a both model look like?
                  5. Empathy across a language barrier. You broke bad news in English. Did Caen and Nagoya students lean on different things — words, silence, tone, body language? What survives the language barrier, and what gets lost?
                  6. What was hard — and was it hard in the same way? Did Caen and Nagoya students find different parts hard (the silence? the family member? saying the diagnosis out loud? not fixing the distress?)? What does that difference tell you?
                  📜 Historical context — how we got here

                  For most of Western medical history, concealing a grave diagnosis was considered good medicine. The 1847 AMA Code of Ethics told doctors to be "the minister of hope and comfort to the sick." As late as 1961, Donald Oken found that about 90% of US physicians preferred not to tell patients they had cancer.

                  The reversal was rapid. Driven by the 1960s–70s rights movements, the rise of bioethics, research scandals such as Tuskegee, and a new emphasis on autonomy and informed consent, a 1979 replication found roughly 97% of physicians now favoured disclosure. France formalised these patient rights later: the loi Kouchner of 4 March 2002 enshrined the right to information, consent and access to one's medical record. Japan's trajectory differed and is unusually well documented: into the early 1990s physicians routinely told only the family, not the patient, of a cancer diagnosis — disclosure then rose sharply through the 1990s, though Japan moved toward selective rather than universal truth-telling, still weighting family wishes heavily.

                  💡 Did you know? In 1961, 9 out of 10 American doctors would not tell a patient they had cancer — within two decades, almost the same proportion said they would.

                  💡 Did you know? Until the 1990s, Japanese physicians often told the family but not the patient — so the "please don't tell my parent" scenario in this roleplay was, until recently, simply standard practice.

                  📚 Guidelines & standards — breaking bad news & disclosure (France · Japan · UK · USA)
                  • France — Loi n° 2002-303 du 4 mars 2002 ("loi Kouchner"), right to be informed and the right not to know (Code de la santé publique, art. L1111-2) legifrance.gouv.fr; HAS, Annoncer une mauvaise nouvelle (2008) has-sante.fr.

                  The cliché "France = autonomy, Japan = family" is half-true at best: France's law also protects the right not to know, and Japan has moved strongly toward patient disclosure. Both systems are negotiating the same tension — and both are still changing.
